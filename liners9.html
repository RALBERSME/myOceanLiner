One day before the passenger steamer Lusitania left New York, a German submarine left Emden. U-20 (click to read more about U-20) , under the command of Lieutenant Commander Walther Schwieger, left the harbor in a northwesterly direction. It was to circle the British Isles to the north and then take up its designated post in the Irish Sea off Liverpool. There it would take up position together with other submarines to prevent the British from bringing more troops and material to the Dardanelles, from where they planned to conquer Istanbul.

U-20 did not expect an easy mission. The Germans were up against an overwhelming enemy at sea - and a warlike one at that. Since the outbreak of war in 1914, Great Britain had used merchant ships or so-called Q-ships, submarine traps, to hunt for submarines. These were old sailing ships that had been converted into decoy ships in Queenstown (hence "Q") by installing a camouflaged gun. They were designed to give the impression that they were unarmed merchant ships

and to destroy German submarines as soon as they appeared to confront the merchant ship.
In February 1915, Churchill also issued a top-secret instruction to British merchant ships to display false national flags to deceive the submarines. If they were nevertheless ordered to turn to, they were to sink the submarine with a ramming blow. There were also plans to set up stern guns.

Great Britain changes the rules of the game

Churchill's methods changed centuries-old rules of naval warfare, which the Germans had also initially adhered to: According to the old prize regulations, no merchant ship could be attacked or sunk without ensuring the safety of the civilian crew and passengers. For their part, merchant ship captains had to tolerate their ships being ordered to turn to or being searched by warships or submarines. The protection of civilians also applied in the event that a merchant ship adhered to the rules - that is, it did not pretend to be a false nationality, nor attempted to ram or use armed resistance. If Great Britain changed the rules of the game like this, was the "Lusitania" still what it claimed to be?

On its way to Liverpool, U-20 had attacked several smaller ships and some freighters.
On the evening of January 6, Lieutenant Commander Schwieger decided not to continue off Queenstown because of fog. The following day, at 2:20 p.m., he sighted a ship with four funnels, a large express steamer. U-20 dived and set off to intercept.

Source: Der Spiegel (German newspaper) dated 23rd Dec. 2008
