After the Lusitania sank


On May 7, 1915, the Lusitania sank. At that time, there were around 1,960 people on board the ship. You will certainly be interested to know what happened to these people. This website is about that.

Of the 1,960 people, only 767 survived, which corresponds to a survival rate of 39.1%.
1,264 of the people were passengers, the rest were part of the crew or band.

At the beginning of the journey we got to know many of these people. You can find out what became of them under "Personal Fates", under "The Prichard Letters" you will find a collection of letters concerning the last moments of second-cabin passenger Richard Preston Prichard, look at reports under "Media" and find out what became of the VIPs of the Lusitania.

Personal Fates:

W.T.Turner


Captain William Thomas Turner

He stayed on the ship's bridge until the water swapped him of the ship. After he floated about three hours in the water, he was rescued.

Serious accusations were made against him. He had put his ship in danger, either negligently or intentionally. He was said not to have sailed in the prescribed zigzag course. The accusations culminated in the Admiralty stating that he had been paid by the Germans to do so.
At the public inquiry, Turner was acquitted of all guilt. However, doubts about this remained and haunted him for the rest of his life.

However, since there was a war and capable captains were needed, he was given other positions. Among other ships, he commanded the Ivernia, with which he was again sunk by a torpedo hit, although this time he had kept to the prescribed zigzag course. He survived this sinking too.

After the war, he retired and returned to Liverpool. Winston Churchill's memoirs brought the war and the sinking back into the headlines, and the press again harassed Turner with accusations of guilt.

In 1929 he fell ill with cancer and died in Liverpool in 1933.

Crew Members

George William Beauchamp

George Beauchamp continued to work at sea for several years. He served as a stoker on Cape Mail ships, among other things.

He later worked as a dock worker in Southampton.

He became friends with Bertram Vere Dean who was the brother of Millvina Dean, the last survivor of the Titanic. He died in April 1965 at the age of 77.

Fate of the passengers

Mrs. Jolivet

Rita Jolivet had just returned to her cabin from lunch when the explosion occurred. She grabbed her lifebuoy, which was in her room, and also a pistol so that she could shoot herself and ease her pain. Then the Lusitania sank and she was washed overboard. She clung to a folding boat. She was later picked up by one of the lifeboats and taken to Queenstown. She continued her career as an actress.
On her deathbed (1971), her last words were “I'm only 77,” but she may have actually been closer to 81.

Mrs. Stevens

Martha Frances Stevens.

Martha Frances Stevens did not survive the sinking of the Lusitania. Her relatives had her body embalmed and sent back to Canada on the ship Hesperia. The Hesperia was also hit by a U-20 torpedo and sunk, along with Martha Frances Stevens' body.

Mrs. Carson

Henrietta McCulloch Carson She died at the age of 19 in the Lusitania's ship accident.

This is what she wrote on April 18th to her sister:
Dearest Nellie this is just to give you the final news. I'm sailing on the Lusitania which leaves New York for Liverpool on May 1st. I had no intention of sailing so large a boat nor of going via New York. But George thinks it is best to take the fastest boat across at this time. You know Nelli that one takes a great risk in crossing the ocean at any time but just now the chances for getting across the ocean safely are fewer still. However we are all in God's hand. And if it is his will I expect I shall reach Liverpool about the 6th of May. It will be great to be home again. We can see that she is travelling second class and she finishes the letter. We shall soon meet again. Fondest love from your loving sister.

Mr. Harris

Dwight Carlton Harris

Dwight Harris had a special life jacket made for him by the sports brand Abercrombie and Fitch. He put it on as soon as the Lusitania reached the war zone. He was on the upper deck when the torpedo hit. He helped his wife Eileen into a lifeboat, which happened to be one of the few that was successfully launched. Then he jumped into the water himself. His luxury life jacket kept him afloat until he was picked up by rescue workers. He and his wife arrived safely in Queenstown. He died in 1969.

Baby Audrey

Audrey Pearl (baby)

When the torpedo hit the ship, Audrey was in her cabin with her brother and the nanny Alice Lines. Alice made her way through the darkness of the ship with the children to the deck. She found a lifeboat that was being loaded and she handed little Stewart into the lifeboat. She and the baby were not allowed to enter the lifeboat because it was full. The lifeboat was launched. Alice jumped into the sea to avoid being separated from Stewart. She had tied the baby around her neck with a scarf. They were pulled out of the water and survived. Audrey died on 11 January 2011 (aged 95). She was the last living Lusitania survivor.
