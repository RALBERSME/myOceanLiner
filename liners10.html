Mid April 2015: Confidental Memo from the Royal Navy to the captains of British steamers:
“War experience has shown that fast steamers can considerably reduce that chance of successful surprise submarine attacks by zigzagging... The underwater speed of a submarine is very slow and it is exceedingly difficult for her to get into position to deliver an attack unless she can observe and predict the course of the ship attacked” (source: Hoehling, 1991, p.63)

April 30th - U-20 left port with 7 torpedoes on board;
it is 210 feet long and 20 feet wide at the center and can travel
15 knots (= 17.3 miles) per hour on the surface and ca. 11 miles per hour under water.

April 30th - U-20 tested his radio signals;
every signal was intercepted by the British Army („Room 40 / London“)

May 2nd - US ambassador at embassy in UK wrote:
„Lord knows when I almost expect such a thing if a British liner full of
American passengers be blown up. What will Uncle Sam do?“

May 3rd - U-20 fired its first torpedo at a ship, but missed it.

May 4th - U-20 fired its second torpedo at a ship, but missed it.

May 5th - U-20 fired its ir third torpedo at
schooner Earl of Lathom successfully ( read more )

May 6th - U-20 fired its fourth torpedo at
British steamer Candidate successfully ( read more )

May 6th - U-20 fired its fifth torpedo at Cargo Liner Centurion successfully,
however it needed its sixth torpedo to sink the Centurion ( read more )

May 6th - U-20 had only fuel for about one more day an only one torpedo left;
according to their guidelines they had to return home when having only
two torpedoes left to ensure to arrive home safely.

May 6th - evening: Captain Turner received a warning message as follows:
„To all British ships, take Liverpool pilot at bar and avoid headlands.
Pass harbours at full speed, steer mid-channel course. Submarines off fastnet”.

May 6th - evening: Captain Turner told on his mandatory socializing event that there was
a warning for tomorrow when they will be entering the war zone (around Ireland / UK).
He assured the passengers that they would then be safe in the hands of the Royal Navy,
which would protect them. He would steam tomorrow at full speed and
forbade passengers from smoking on board at night or carrying light sources.

May 6th - night: Although some passengers are worried, not all are concerned,
as they trust in the Royal Navy and the speed of the Lusitania.
Passenger Isaac Lehman prefers to spend the night on deck in everyday clothes
to be prepared for anything.

May 7th - short after breakfast:
Colonel House (US diplomat) talkst to Sir Edward Grey (British Foreign Secretary)
who will later meet the British King George and asks him: „What will America do
if the Germany sink an ocean liner with American passengers on board?“ House replies: „That would carry the United States into the war. I will ask King George this question as well.“

May 7th - Captain Turner gives order to keep course ort he Old Head of Kinsale (Ireland);
this maneuver will take about 40 minutes and requires an exact straight course while keeping
the same speed to make the calculations.

May 7th - The moment Turner gives order for Old Head of Kinsale, U-20 notices the Lusitania
(not knowing which steamer it is); entry from U-20 log book:
„Right ahead appear four funnels and two masts of a steamer with the course vertical to us.
Ship is made out tob e a large passenger steamer“ (source: Hoehling, 1991, p.16); U-20

May 7th - U-20 stays underwater to avoid being seen and sets course on the Lusitania.

May 7th - Captain Turner makes course adjustment and turns into the direction of U-20
(not being aware of U-20)

May 7th - On board of Lusitania Jay Foster Stackhouse (British explorer) explains to Harold Bolto (aged 23) why this ship could never be torpedoed.

May 7th - U-20 is 700 metres away from Lusitania and fires its last torpedo.
The torpodeo has a speed of 38 knots per hour.

May 7th - On Lusitania Leslie Morton is the first who notices the torpedo coming.
He yells into his megaphone“Torpedo coming on the starboard side”.
